Explainable Graph Neural Networks for Drug–Drug Interaction Prediction: A Faithfulness-Evaluated Study over Clinical Knowledge Graphs
Master's Thesis Abstract ~300 words IMRaD structure

Background Drug–drug interactions (DDIs) are a leading cause of preventable adverse drug reactions, posing significant risks in polypharmacy patients who concurrently use multiple medications. Recent advances in graph neural networks (GNNs) have enabled accurate prediction of polypharmacy side effects by modelling the complex relationships between drugs, protein targets, and adverse events as heterogeneous knowledge graphs. Despite achieving high predictive performance, these models function as clinical black boxes — offering no mechanistic rationale for their predictions — thereby limiting their adoption in pharmacovigilance and clinical decision support.

Objective This thesis presents the first systematic study of GNN explanation methods applied specifically to the DDI side effect prediction task. We address three research questions: whether existing post-hoc explanation methods produce faithful subgraph explanations for heterogeneous DDI knowledge graphs; how these methods compare on quantitative faithfulness, stability, and pharmacological validity criteria; and whether the resulting explanations are interpretable and useful to pharmacists in practice.

Methods We construct a reproducible heterogeneous knowledge graph integrating DrugBank, TWOSIDES, SIDER, and STRING protein–protein interaction data, spanning 645 drugs, 964 polypharmacy side effect types, and 19,089 protein nodes. A Graph Attention Network (GAT) is trained on this graph for multi-label side effect prediction, with Decagon as the primary baseline. Four explanation methods — raw attention rollout, GNNExplainer, PGExplainer, and the k-hop Explanation with Convolutional Core (KEC) — are applied to 200 sampled drug pairs. Faithfulness is evaluated using sufficiency and necessity perturbation tests; stability is assessed under repeated edge dropout. Pharmacological validity is verified by cross-referencing highlighted protein nodes against known shared CYP450 enzyme targets in DrugBank. Finally, a task-based usability study with pharmacy students examines whether the best-performing explanation supports correct mechanism identification.

Expected contributions This work provides: (1) a publicly released, leakage-free DDI knowledge graph pipeline; (2) the first faithfulness benchmark of GNN explanation methods for polypharmacy prediction; (3) evidence on whether raw attention constitutes a reliable explanation in this setting; and (4) qualitative findings from pharmacist evaluation of GNN-generated interaction explanations. Results are expected to demonstrate that attention-based explanations are insufficiently faithful, that KEC outperforms perturbation-based methods on sufficiency, and that subgraph explanations grounded in shared protein targets are interpretable to domain experts. These findings will inform the design of trustworthy, clinically deployable DDI prediction systems.
