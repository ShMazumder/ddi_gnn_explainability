One-sentence pitch
DDI prediction models achieve strong accuracy but are clinical black boxes — this thesis is the first systematic study of whether current GNN explanation methods produce faithful, chemically grounded subgraph explanations for polypharmacy side effect prediction.
XAI / Explainability Drug Safety Knowledge Graphs Graph Neural Networks Polypharmacy
DDIs are responsible for a significant share of adverse drug reactions. Models like Decagon predict polypharmacy side effects with high AUROC, but clinicians cannot verify why a drug pair is flagged — which shared protein target, which metabolic pathway, which chemical substructure drives the prediction. This thesis fills that gap by systematically applying and evaluating explanation methods on DDI-specific KG-GNNs, with rigorous faithfulness measurement and a small pharmacist usability study.

Why this gap exists — and why now

The DDI prediction literature splits cleanly into two tracks that have never been combined properly: high-accuracy GNN models and XAI methods for molecular GNNs. The following five gaps define the thesis's contribution space.
1

No explanation study exists on polypharmacy KG-GNNs

GNNExplainer and PGExplainer have been applied to molecule property prediction and drug-protein binding, but not to the DDI-specific task of polypharmacy side effect prediction on heterogeneous KGs (drugs + proteins + side effects). The graph structure is fundamentally different — edge types represent specific side effects, not bonds — making direct transfer of existing explanations non-trivial.

2

Explanation faithfulness is not evaluated in the biomedical GNN literature

A 2025 Frontiers systematic review explicitly states that integrating GNNExplainer / PGExplainer with DDI models is future work. No existing DDI paper measures explanation faithfulness (sufficiency / necessity) — they either skip evaluation entirely or use qualitative visualisations. This thesis provides the first quantitative evaluation protocol for DDI explanation faithfulness.

3

Cutoff selection for subgraph explanations is unsolved

GNNExplainer and PGExplainer produce edge importance scores but require a threshold to extract a subgraph explanation. In drug contexts, an arbitrary cutoff may produce chemically invalid substructures. No DDI study proposes a principled cutoff method grounded in pharmacological validity.

4

Attention ≠ explanation, but this has not been tested for DDI models

For GAT-based DDI models, raw attention weights are often used as pseudo-explanations. However, the "attention is not explanation" literature (Jain & Wallace 2019) shows this is unreliable. Whether attention faithfully reflects DDI predictions on heterogeneous KGs is untested.

5

No clinician or pharmacist usability study exists for DDI GNN explanations

XAI research in other medical domains (radiology, pathology) routinely includes clinician studies. DDI explanation papers produce heatmaps but never ask whether a pharmacist can use them to understand or verify a prediction. Even a small (n=5–10) study would be novel.

Proposed chapter structure

Ch. 1Introduction & motivation~15 pages
Clinical problem: polypharmacy and ADRs. Computational approaches to DDI prediction and their accuracy–interpretability tradeoff. Research questions: (1) Do current explanation methods produce faithful subgraph explanations for KG-based DDI GNNs? (2) Can we rank explanation methods by faithfulness and pharmacological validity? (3) Are these explanations useful to clinicians?
Ch. 2Background & related work~25 pages
DDI biology (pharmacokinetics vs pharmacodynamics interactions). Knowledge graphs in pharmacology (DrugBank, SIDER, TWOSIDES). GNN architectures for DDI (Decagon, GAT-DDI, MRGNN). GNN explanation methods: GNNExplainer, PGExplainer, attention rollout, KEC, SLGNN. Faithfulness evaluation frameworks. Related XAI in biomedicine.
Ch. 3DDI knowledge graph constructionCore contribution
Construct a clean, reproducible heterogeneous KG from DrugBank (drug nodes, 86 interaction types), TWOSIDES (964 polypharmacy side effects, 645 drugs, 46k pairs), SIDER (mono side effects), and STRING (protein–protein interactions). Define train/val/test splits to avoid data leakage. Document the pipeline fully — this alone is a reusable contribution.
Deliverable: public KG pipeline on GitHub
Ch. 4GAT-based DDI prediction modelCore contribution
Train a Graph Attention Network (GAT) on the constructed KG for multi-label polypharmacy side effect prediction. Reproduce Decagon as the baseline. Demonstrate competitive or improved AUROC / AUPRC. Analyse attention head diversity. This chapter establishes the model whose explanations are evaluated in Chapter 5.
Deliverable: trained GAT model + reproducibility scripts
Ch. 5Explanation method comparison & faithfulness evaluationPrimary contribution
Apply four explanation methods to the GAT: (1) raw attention weights, (2) GNNExplainer, (3) PGExplainer, (4) KEC (k-hop explanation). For each, extract subgraph explanations for 200 sampled drug pairs. Evaluate faithfulness via sufficiency (does the subgraph alone reproduce the prediction?) and necessity (does removing it degrade it?). Compare stability under input perturbation. Test whether explanations correspond to known pharmacological mechanisms (CYP450 enzymes, shared protein targets) as a validity check.
Deliverable: faithfulness evaluation toolkit + results table
Ch. 6Pharmacist usability studyNovel angle
Design a task-based study (n=6–10 pharmacists or pharmacy students). Show participants explanations from the best-performing method alongside predictions. Measure: (1) agreement with predicted interaction type, (2) ability to identify the flagged mechanism, (3) trust calibration. Analyse qualitatively. Even a small study distinguishes this thesis from purely computational work.
Deliverable: study protocol + qualitative findings
Ch. 7Discussion & conclusion~20 pages
Which explanation method works best for KG-based DDI prediction, and why. Limitations: incomplete KGs, class imbalance for rare side effects, size of usability study. Future work: inductive explanations for new drugs, LLM-generated natural language explanations from subgraphs, integration into clinical decision support.

Key experiments

E1 KG construction & baseline reproduction ⌄
Goal

Reproduce Decagon AUROC on the TWOSIDES split. Establish clean train/val/test splits with no leakage. Validate KG statistics match published figures.

Inputs

TWOSIDES (964 side effects, 645 drugs), DrugBank 5.x, STRING v12 protein interactions.

Expected metric

AUROC ≥ 0.87 (Decagon published figure), AUPRC > 0.75.

E2 GAT training for polypharmacy side effect prediction ⌄
Goal

Train a GAT with multi-head attention on the heterogeneous DDI KG. Compare AUROC, AUPRC, and training time against Decagon. Analyse which side effect types benefit most from attention.

Architecture

3-layer GAT, 8 attention heads, edge-type-conditioned message passing. Negative sampling weighted by side effect frequency.

Ablations

Remove protein nodes; remove SIDER mono side effects; 1 vs 8 heads.

E3 Attention-as-explanation faithfulness test ⌄
Goal

Test whether raw GAT attention weights are faithful explanations. For 200 drug pairs, compute attention rollout subgraphs. Measure sufficiency (prediction preserved when only attending nodes kept) and necessity (prediction degrades when they are removed).

Hypothesis

Attention will show low necessity scores — i.e. attention ≠ faithful explanation — replicating Jain & Wallace (2019) for the DDI setting.

Metric

Sufficiency score, necessity score, JS divergence between full-model and subgraph-model output distributions.

E4 GNNExplainer vs PGExplainer vs KEC comparison ⌄
Goal

Run three post-hoc explanation methods on the same 200 drug pairs. Measure faithfulness, stability under 10% edge dropout, and runtime. Rank by a composite score.

Validity check

For the top 50 explained drug pairs, manually verify whether the highlighted protein nodes correspond to known shared CYP450 enzyme targets in DrugBank — a pharmacologically grounded ground truth proxy.

Expected finding

KEC (provably maximises faithfulness) should outperform GNNExplainer/PGExplainer on sufficiency; PGExplainer should be more stable across instances.

E5 Pharmacist usability study ⌄
Design

Present 6–10 pharmacy students with 10 drug pairs each. For each pair, show: (a) the predicted side effect, (b) a subgraph explanation from the best E4 method. Ask: "Does this explanation help you understand why?" (5-pt Likert) and "Can you identify the interaction mechanism?" (free text, then coded).

Ethics

Pharmacy student participants (not patients). Minimal risk — survey only. Requires university ethics board approval (~4 weeks). Recruit via pharmacy department.

Output

Qualitative coding of free-text responses. Likert distribution. Quote-supported discussion in thesis.
